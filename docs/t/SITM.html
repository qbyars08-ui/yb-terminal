Thesis

I sold my Nokia to buy a clock. 395 employees, revenue up 88 percent in a quarter, and it makes the one part almost no investor has ever thought about: the timing chip that gives every chip in an AI datacenter its heartbeat. Most controversial trade I have made all year, and I think one of the best.

The bottleneck is real. An AI cluster is not one computer, it is tens of thousands of GPUs that have to behave like one, passing data over links running 800 gigabits per second with signaling at 224 gigabits per lane. At those speeds the clock cannot wobble. Wobble has a name, jitter, and when jitter creeps up, bits get read at the wrong instant, links throw errors and retrain, and the most expensive hardware ever built sits idle waiting for late data. An idle GPU is money on fire. The cheapest-sounding part in the room decides how fast the most expensive part runs. Small in dollars, enormous in consequence.

The old way is breaking. Timing meant quartz for a hundred years, and an AI hall is a terrible place to be a quartz crystal: hot, electrically noisy, vibrating from thousands of fans and pumps. Quartz drifts with temperature, ages, and hates vibration, exactly as the requirements get harder. This is the same shape as Wolfspeed, which is why I bought both the same week. In power, silicon hit a wall and silicon carbide took over. In timing, quartz is hitting a wall and silicon is taking over.

SiTime owns the silicon version. MEMS resonators etched from silicon plus a custom analog chip: stable across temperature, shrugs off vibration, and programmable to almost any frequency, which collapses a customer's parts list into one flexible product. For the AI datacenter they built Chorus, the first fully integrated clock generator built for AI datacenters, and Epoch, an oscillator that holds time roughly twice as accurately as quartz after eight hours of drift. Holding time better is not a luxury when thousands of accelerators have to stay synchronized. It is throughput.

The receipts: FY25 revenue $326.7M, up 61 percent. Then it accelerated: Q1 FY26 at $113.6M, up more than 88 percent. The datacenter segment did $75.7M in the quarter, up 158 percent, seven straight quarters above 100 percent growth. Guide is at least 80 percent for the year. Then they pressed the bet and agreed to buy Renesas' timing business, about $300M of revenue at 70 percent gross margin within a year of closing. A 395-person company growing 88 percent is buying a business as big as itself.

The Nokia swap, stated plainly: NOK is a real AI-infrastructure play (optical, DCI, IP routing, an ex-Intel datacenter CEO) but it is a $73B battleship with 78,000 employees where AI is a thin slice of a slow base. SiTime is 395 people where the AI datacenter is the entire story. I am 17, my horizon is decades, and I am compounding where the buildout is most constrained. Sold the safety, bought the slope.
