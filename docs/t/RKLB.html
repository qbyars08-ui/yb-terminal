Thesis

Rocket Lab is a vertically-integrated space company with three revenue streams:

1. Electron launch — small-satellite launches at high cadence. Second most active US launch provider after SpaceX. Cost-effective for sub-300kg payloads. 2. Neutron launch (in development) — medium-lift reusable rocket targeting H2'26 first flight. The capability bet — moves RKLB from small-sat niche into medium-lift competition with SpaceX Falcon 9. 3. Spacecraft components and complete satellites — Photon spacecraft platform, satellite components for primes (Lockheed, Northrop), national security customers.

The Neutron program is the inflection point. Successful Neutron launch + ramp moves RKLB from a $50-100M-quarter launch business to a multi-billion-dollar-revenue medium-lift competitor with structural cost advantages from reusability.

The non-launch revenue (spacecraft + components) provides a steady-cash floor while Neutron develops, reducing reliance on launch-market timing.

Kill vectors (Predator checks)

1. Neutron slip — H2'26 first flight is the public commitment. Any meaningful slip (multi-quarter) re-rates the medium-lift competitive position downward and pushes the cash flow inflection. 2. Electron cadence drop — small-sat launch is the cash-generating side today. If Electron cadence drops materially (engine failures, customer demand softening), the floor under the company weakens. 3. Spacecraft margin compression — competition for spacecraft components and complete satellites is intensifying. If RKLB has to take lower-margin work to keep the backlog filled, segment economics weaken. 4. Capital raise overhang — Neutron development is capital-intensive. Continued dilutive raises pressure the share price even with execution on track. 5. SpaceX competition compression — Starship's reusable mega-lift capacity, if it ramps, can disrupt the medium-lift category before Neutron reaches scale.
