Thesis

Ouster is a digital lidar manufacturer with a full perception stack covering automotive, industrial, smart infrastructure, and robotics customer verticals.

The bet:

  • Digital lidar over analog lidar. Ouster's CMOS-based architecture has structural cost-and-scale advantages over older analog-photon-counting designs.
  • Full perception stack. Not just sensors — complete perception software (detection, tracking, classification) bundled with hardware. This shifts customer relationships from "buy a part" to "buy a system" with stickier margins.
  • REV8 launch. The latest sensor generation, launched recently. Improved range, resolution, cost. Customers re-evaluate their lidar choice each generation; REV8 is the product that wins or loses share for the next 2-3 years.
  • Multi-vertical customer base. Robotaxi (Aurora, Motional, Cruise post-restart), industrial automation, smart-city infrastructure (traffic, parking), drones, robotics. Diversification reduces single-vertical risk.

Kill vectors (Predator checks)

1. Robotaxi TAM stagnation — robotaxi is a major demand driver for high-resolution lidar. If the robotaxi rollout pace stalls (regulatory, technology, economics), OUST's growth trajectory in the auto vertical compresses. 2. Perception commodity compression — if open-source perception stacks (Apollo, Autoware) close the software-margin advantage, OUST becomes a sensor commodity. Hardware-only economics are tougher. 3. REV8 execution slip — sensor-generation launches are pivot points for customer commitment. Yield issues, performance disappointments, or supply-chain delays during the REV8 ramp lose share. 4. Customer vertical loss — losing a major program (e.g., a robotaxi platform standardizing on a competitor like Innoviz, Luminar, or Hesai) compresses revenue. 5. Hesai competition (China) — Hesai is the China-domiciled lidar leader with cost advantages and scale. Geographic and procurement-policy dynamics protect some of OUST's market, but global competition is real.
