Thesis

VST is the largest US nuclear fleet outside Constellation (CEG), with deregulated-market exposure and operational scale that's unreplicable on any short-to-medium time horizon.

The bet is structurally simple: AI infrastructure needs power. The grid is constrained. New gas takes years to permit and build. New nuclear takes a decade plus. Existing nuclear that's already running, already permitted, and already operating at high capacity factor is the rarest asset in the AI buildout.

Two long-dated contracts anchor the revenue:

  • 20-year Microsoft PPA — multi-GW capacity commitment supplying behind-the-meter power for AI datacenters.
  • 20-year Meta PPA — second long-dated commitment, similar structure.
  • Combined: decades of committed revenue against an asset (nuclear capacity) that's appreciating in value as AI demand grows.

The deregulated-market exposure (ERCOT in Texas + PJM in the Northeast) means VST sells power into competitive wholesale markets at market clearing prices, which have re-rated structurally higher with AI demand. The PPAs provide the floor; the merchant exposure provides the upside.

Why anchor

VST passes the manifesto signature-line test:

"I buy the exact raw materials, hardware machines, and power plants that force artificial intelligence to actually exist and operate in the real world."

Power plants are literally in the signature line. VST owns nuclear power plants — the highest-quality, most reliable, most capacity-factor-efficient generation asset in the buildout. Without VST-class capacity, AI datacenters cannot scale at the pace that's being committed.

Kill vectors (Predator checks)

1. NRC ruling against behind-the-meter — there's an active regulatory question about whether nuclear plants can sell power directly to a co-located datacenter (behind-the-meter) versus through the grid. A negative ruling would invalidate the structure of the Microsoft and Meta PPAs and force a renegotiation at less favorable terms. 2. ERCOT / PJM policy shift — wholesale power market design changes (capacity payments, ancillary services rules, transmission rate updates) can compress the merchant side of the P&L materially. 3. Gas price collapse — VST has gas plants alongside nuclear. A persistent natural gas price collapse (oversupply, demand drop) reduces gas-plant margins; nuclear is the durable side. 4. Microsoft / Meta PPA cancellation — less likely than the regulatory risk because long-dated PPAs have strong contractual frameworks, but still a vector. Both customers also have the optionality to defer. 5. Capacity-add at scale — if behind-the-meter nuclear becomes a generic asset class and many smaller nuclear operators sign similar PPAs, VST's relative scarcity premium compresses.
