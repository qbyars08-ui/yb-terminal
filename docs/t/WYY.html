Thesis

One of my larger positions is a $126 million company almost no analyst covers. WidePoint runs the identity, credentialing, and mobile-device management that lets federal employees securely get into systems and buildings. Technology Management as a Service, sold to the US government, one of a small number of providers certified to handle that kind of federal identity work, and it has been doing it since the 1990s. Boring, sticky, mission-critical plumbing. Exactly what I like to own.

Straight up: I bought WYY before I wrote the thesis, which breaks my own rule. This page is the homework I owed myself, written after it already ran. The thesis holds, which is why I am still holding.

Why now: the entire government is being pushed toward zero-trust security, where every login and every device gets verified continuously, no exceptions. That mandate runs straight into WidePoint's core business. Identity is the new security perimeter, and the federal government is the most demanding identity customer on the planet. When the rule flips from trust the network to verify every person and device, the certified company already doing the work does not have to win the customer. It already has it.

The setup is invisibility. $126 million cap, about 240 employees, the less-watched NYSE American, barely any coverage. It already ran from under $3 to almost $13, which tells me the contracts are starting to show up in the numbers and the market is just beginning to notice. When a microcap with real federal certifications and sticky recurring revenue starts to move, it can go a long way before Wall Street even builds a model on it. Early and uncomfortable beats late and confident.

Not chasing it higher. Holding what I own, sized as a high-conviction microcap, not a place to park money I need.
