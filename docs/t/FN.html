Thesis

Every AI data center runs on light. Once you put tens of thousands of GPUs in a building, copper runs out of reach and speed in a few meters. The only way to connect that many chips is fiber and optical transceivers. And optical is the one component that scales faster than the compute itself. Double the GPUs and you add far more than double the optical links, because every new chip has to talk to all the others. The roadmap runs 800G today, 1.6T next, co-packaged optics after that.

The market piled into the loud optical names. Coherent at 12 times sales. Lumentum and AAOI at 30. Here is what most people miss: those brands do not build most of their own hardware. They design the module and hand it to a contract manufacturer. The biggest of those in optical, by a wide margin, is Fabrinet. It runs its factories out of Thailand, does the ultra-precision assembly of the actual 800G and 1.6T transceivers, and trades at 5 times sales. The cheapest name in the whole group. Do not buy the optical stock. Buy the factory behind it.

Stop on the 12% gross margin, because that is the whole misread. People see 12% next to Coherent at 37% and call it low quality. Backwards. The thin margin is the moat. This is brutal, capital-heavy, precision-execution work. The brands could bring it in house, but it would crush their own margins and tie up capital they would rather spend on design and sales. A company earning 20% return on equity out of a 12% margin business has made itself the cheapest, most reliable option for work nobody else wants to own.

Two catalysts, both pointing the same way. Fabrinet finished qualification and started shipping two 800G datacom programs straight to a hyperscaler, cutting out the brand in the middle. That ramp builds through fiscal 2027, and it is a different, bigger lane than being a subcontractor. Second, on May 31 the company put roughly $32 million into a ~14% stake in a Taiwan advanced wafer-level packaging firm. That is a paid seat at the co-packaged optics table, and they are already running CPO programs with three customers.

The honest part the bulls skip: datacom, the AI piece, grew just 4% Y/Y last quarter and shrank sequentially. The growth came from telecom, up 55% to a record. But read why. Management said datacom demand exceeded what they could ship. They could not get enough lasers, memory, and ASICs to build units already ordered. That is the single best problem a supplier can have. When those inputs loosen, that backlog ships.

It fits the spine. I already hold the memory, the brain, and the compute. Fabrinet is the wiring between them.

What Kills It

  • cpo-eats-discrete-transceivers: if optics move onto the chip package faster than expected, the discrete transceiver volume Fabrinet lives on shrinks, and the Taiwan stake is a hedge, not a guarantee.
  • customer-concentration-shock: a handful of brands and hyperscalers drive most of the revenue, so one in-sourcing decision or one lost program leaves a mark.
  • brand-insourcing: the moat depends on brands not wanting this work; if one decides the margin math flipped, volume walks.
  • ai-capex-scare-derating: 54x trailing earnings on a stock that already tripled off its lows inside a year can drop 20% on any AI-capex scare.
  • datacom-supply-chain-stall: the backlog thesis assumes lasers, memory, and ASICs loosen; if the input shortage drags, the AI leg keeps not driving the bus.
